A brief history of The Met. Office

The Met. Office, formal title, The Meteorological Office, was formed as a small department within the Board of Trade in 1854, under the command of Admiral FitzRoy, to provide meteorological and sea current information to mariners. By 1861, a sufficient number of regular observations were being received by telegraph from Great Britain and France to permit the issue of storm warnings to ports and forecasts to the press. These services were stopped in 1866 on the recommendation of a Royal Society committee. Storm warnings were rapidly reinstated but published forecasts did not reappear until 1879.

About this time, the first international meteorological congress in Vienna established an International Meteorological Organization to further essential international co-operation. This eventually transformed into the World Meteorological Organization (WMO), a specialized agency of the United Nations.

Rapid developments both within and without meteorology led to the establishment of the first outstation at South Farnborough in 1912 to give advice to pilots. During the Great War, meteorological services were developed in the separate parts of the armed forces but in 1920, all four were combined into one organization under the auspices of the Air Ministry, a step which has been of enormous benefit ever since. Services rapidly grew and spread through the Empire.

Forecasts were first broadcast by the BBC in 1922 and captions were shown on TV in 1936. In 1937, the Admiralty took over the weather service for the Navy. As well as the start of World War II, 1939 saw the introduction of a system for obtaining data from the upper air by 'radiosonde', balloon-borne sensors transmitting pressure, temperature and humidity data to receiving sites on land. There was a huge increase of staff during this era, numbers rising to 6900. The Meteorological Research Flight was formed in 1942 and has performed valuable work ever since, providing detailed measurements of a wide range of atmospheric parameters from aircraft.

After the war, a Directorate of Research was formed to control and direct the practical and theoretical investigations that were being carried out. The first live TV weather forecasts started in 1954 (presenters had five minutes in those days!) and London Weather Centre opened in 1959.

The modern era can be said to have arrived in 1962 when an electronic computer was installed at our new HQ in Bracknell. In 1964, the first operational cloud pictures from satellites became available. Research and development in both these fields, much of it carried out within the Office, have transformed the science of meteorology and will continue to do so.

1981 saw the installation of our first supercomputer, the CDC Cyber 205, and the introduction of our 15-level atmospheric computer model. The Cyber was replaced by a Cray Y-MP machine in 1990, allowing the introduction of a new 19-level model and improved representation of atmospheric processes.

A second Cray Y-MP was installed in 1991 to support the climate research programme carried out at the jointly-funded (Met. Office/DoE) Hadley Centre. Both machines were replaced by a single Cray C90 in May 1994 with a resultant six-fold increase in the speed of processing.

The Met. Office became an MoD Executive Agency in April 1990. A month later, the Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research was opened. The Met. Office remains a centre of excellence for the development of the science of meteorology and the provision of weather-related services to a wide range of customers.
